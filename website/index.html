The Idea Behind the Game

Ups and Downs is a fun game revolving around the social stigma and taboo around failure in our society. As a rapidly progressing society, we place a great emphasis on success and always being successful in what we do, but very rarely do we acknowledge failure as an important part of this journey. We teach our kids that they should always be successful and avoid failure, but we don’t teach them that failure is a precursor to success. It is not a detour from the road; instead, it is part of the road, though it may be a minor bump. Most people fear failure and run away from it when they should actually be embracing it.

This game has been desgined for anyone who has ever felt pressured to be perfect or has been discouraged by failure. It is for people who feel like they can’t succeed in life if they make mistakes or fail a couple of times—basically everyone. Players of all ages can use this game, as it is intuitive and simple!

About the Game

Ups and Downs is a game that is designed to teach people about the importance of failure. Although this is to be discovered throughout the course of the game, the way a player ”wins” is by following an unconventional path. This path is modeled along the way a player would “fail” in most other games, but in this game, we reward the player for “failing”: falling down a hole, driving off the road, running into an enemy, etc. The game itself will be much like any other standard games (Mario Kart, Ovenbreak, Subway Surfers, etc.) but instead of emphasizing the need to complete the game perfectly, we will have “holes” and alternative paths for when you “fail” (some examples of said holes include falling down a hole, driving off the road, running into an enemy, or anything else that would otherwise be detrimental to the player in the normal game). Of course, for the sake of a new experience each time and to emphasize the overall message, not all “holes” will lead to a different path; some will simply result in death.

About the Creators

This project was designed as the final project for a Girls Who Code Summer Immersion Program, which was hosted at Workday. The four developers are Smruthi Balajee, Alion Fang, Mahika Nayak, and Jesscia Sanborn. We are all rising juniors and seniors in various high schools around the Bay Area. We were all united by a passion for learning and a will to change the world, one line of code at a time.

About Girls Who Code

Girls Who Code is a nonprofit organization which aims to support and increase the number of women in computer science. The organization runs summer programs which teach computing and programming skills to high school girls.Girls Who Code was founded by Reshma Saujani in 2012, when she noticed that schools along her campaign route lacked girls in computer science classrooms. The organization runs summer programs teaching high school girls computing skills like programming, robotics, and web design.
